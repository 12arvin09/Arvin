In this project, I tried to build a Brain-Computer Interface headset that detects letters people are thinking, using their brainwaves. I used an EEG sensor-based approach because it's non-invasive and can read signals through contact with the scalp. Since buying a ready-made OpenBCI headset from the official store was way too expensive, I tried to create it on a low budget here in Mongolia. Fortunately, all of this became possible thanks to the amazing engineer brothers and mentors at ONDOSPACE.The EEG sensors were brought from the OpenBCI store, and the headset was 3D printed using open-source files from OpenBCI’s GitHub, with the help of engineer Mr. Budragchaa. The Cyton board was printed in Shenzhen, thanks to Mr. Tsolmon Yadmaa’s help. For the software, I used OpenViBE instead of OpenBCI’s official app. I focused on the P300 speller method, where users look at flashing letters in a grid. When the target letter flashes, the brain gives off a signal called the P300 wave. The system reads those waves, learns the patterns, and tries to match the letter you're focusing on.In the end, the accuracy improved from 72% to 82% after a lot of training and repeating the process. Even though it’s TRULY far from accurate, I’m honestly happy for the result. I had many limitations, both in tools and in my own knowledge. But building something that responds to brain signals gave me a whole new sense of what’s possible. It made me want to keep going, keep learning, and see how far this connection between mind and machine can go.
