My journey in Sukhbaatar province ‘beginning on a stage in front of 300 children’ and ‘later teaching in a room less than 10 square meters with just three students’ was filled with moments of deep understanding.When I was younger, teaching and sharing what I knew felt more like a hobby. I was content simply talking about what I understood. But stepping into the role of a teacher, earning the trust of students and filling gaps in their knowledge, brought me meaningful sense of responsibility.I learned to see familiar topics through unfamiliar eyes. I let go of methods I would grown comfortable with and replaced them with new approaches that I hadnt considered before. I broke away from the familiar and put myself in every possible teaching scenario, so I could give back something real and personal to my students. That, to me, was the greatest value of the experience.That’s why every lesson was more than just solving math problems. To help them concentrate, I played Oppenheimer’s soundtrack in the background. I added memes to lesson slides to make learning fun. At night, I stayed up correcting every notebook by hand, writing personal comments until my fingers cramped. I dedicated my days to being the kind of teacher I would’ve wanted : creative, present, and committed.That is why my time teaching at Tomujin Academy stands out as the period where I put in the most effort, worked the hardest, embraced the weight of responsibility, and grew more than ever before.
