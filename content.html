Biggest Takeaway : Failing fast taught me more than succeeding quickly.

From a young age, I have always wanted to tell people what I learned. I wanted to immediately share something that made me go “Wow!”, hoping others feel the same excitement and learn something too. Following that instinct, I ended up teaching for a while, and later on, I found myself creating content to share knowledge with even more people. You could call it one of my side passions.But as I kept making these short pieces of content, which took a lot of time and effort, I started asking myself bigger questions. What I am sharing is truly helpful or valuable to other people’s live? Was my understanding accurate? Was I explaining things correctly? And most importantly, was it right to present all these informations as fact? Instead of just focusing on what I knew, I began to feel the weight of sharing knowledge with others.For the first time, I felt the responsibility that comes with filling someone else’s gaps in understanding through something I created. For example, I remember spending two to three days researching just to make a 90-second video explaining the Mongolian Lunar New Year calendar. I read some books by great Mongolian astrologers, quoted articles from respected professors’ article, and searched through countless sources online. In the end, all that effort was compressed into a short video but what mattered most was making sure every word was true, and every piece of information is expressed as valuable to others.That’s when I really understood the importance of sharing knowledge responsibly. And I’ll probably continue this journey in the future. Because sharing isn’t just something I do and it’s part of who I am.
